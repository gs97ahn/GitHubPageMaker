Computer Network - The Network Core

1️⃣ Packet Switching

  • Messages: Anything the application designer wants which are exchanged by end systems.
    • May contain data such as an e-mail message, a JPEG image, or an MP3 audio file.
  • Packets: A format which a long messages are broken down to.
  • Packet switches: A tool that packet travels.
    • There are two predominant types of packet switches: routers and link-layer switches
    • Packets are transmitted over each communication link at a rate equal to full transmission rate of link.
    • e.g. Source system or a packet switch is sending a packet of L bits over a link with transmission rate R bit/sec -> packet is transmitted at L/R seconds

Store-and-Forward Transmission

  • Store-and-forward transmission: The packet switch must receive the entire packet before it can begin to transmit the first bit of the packet onto the outbound link.
    • To explore in more detail, consider a simple network consisting of two end systems connected by a single router, as shown below. A router will typically have many incident links, since its job is to switch an incoming packet onto an ongoing link; in this simple example, the router has the rather simple task of transferring a packet from one (input) link to the only other attached link. In this example, the source has three packets, each consisting of L bits, to send to the destination. At the snapshot of the time shown below, the source has transmitted some of packet 1, and the front of packet 1 has already arrived at the router. Because the router employs store-and-forwarding, at this instant of time, the router cannot transmit the bits it has received; instead it must first buffer the packet’s bits. Only after the router has received all of the packet’s bits can it begin to transmit the packet onto the outbound link. To gain some insight into store-and-forward transmission, let’s now calculate the amount of time that elapses from when the source begins to send the packet until the destination has received the entire packet. The source begins to transmit at time 0; at time L/R seconds, the source has transmitted the entire packet, and the entire packet has been received and stored at the router. At time L/R seconds, since the router has just received and stored at the router. At time L/R seconds, since the router has just received the entire packet, it can begin to transmit the packet onto the outbound link towards the destination; at time 2L/R, the router has transmitted the entire packet, and the entire packet has been received by the destination. Thus, the total delay is 2L/R.

Queuing Delays and Packet Loss

  • Output buffer(a.k.a. output queue): Stores packets that the router is about to send into that link.
  • Queuing delays: Variable and depend on the level of congestion in the network.
  • Packet loss: The arriving packet or one of already-queued packets will be dropped since the buffer is already completely full.

Forwarding Tables and Routing Protocols

Forwarding table: Maps destination addresses to that router’s outbound links. Routing protocols: Used to automatically set the forwarding tables.

2️⃣ Circuit Switching

There are 2 fundamental approaches to moving data through a network of links and switches: circuit switching and packet switching.

Tip❗️

Circuit-switched network Packet-switched network
Resources needed along a path to provide communication between the end systems are reserved for commuication between the end systems Resources needed along a path to provide communication between the end systems are not reserved for commuication between the end systems

End-to-end connection: The network must first reserve one circuit on each of two links.

Multiplexing in Circuit-Switched Networks

A circuit in a link is implemented with either frequency-division multiplexing(FDM) or time-division multiplexing(TDM).

  • Frequency-division multiplexing(FDM): The frequency spectrum of a link is divided up among the connections established across the link.
  • Time-division multiplexing(TDM): Time is divided of fixed duration and each frame is divided into a fixed number of time slots.
  • bandwidth: The width of the frequency band.

  • silent period: A period of silent in circuit switching when network resources are allocated to the connection but are not used.

3️⃣ A Network of Networks

Network Structure 1

Interconnects all of the access ISPs with a single global transit ISP.

  • Customer: The access ISP
  • Provider: The global transit ISP

Network Structure 2

Two-tier hierarchy with global transit providers residing at the top tier and access ISPs at the bottom tier.

  • Regional ISP: The access ISPs in the region connect.
  • Tier-1 ISPs: Similar to (imaginary) global transit ISP.
    • e.g. Level 3 Communications, AT&T, Sprint, and NTT.

Tip ❗️

There is a customer-provider relationship at each level of the hierarchy. The tier-1 ISPs do not pay anyone as they are at the top of the hierarchy.

Network Structure 3

Multi-tier hierarchy, which is still only a crude approximation of today’s Internet.

  • Points of Presence(PoPs): A group of one or more routers in the provider’s network where customer ISPs can connect into the provider ISP.
  • Multi-home Connect two or more provider ISPs.
  • Peer: A pair of nearby ISPs at the same level of hierarchy, directly connecting their networks together so that all traffic between them passes over the direct connection to reduce the cost.
  • Internet ExchangePoint(IXP): A meeting point where multiple ISPs can peer together.

Network Structure 4

Ecosystem consisting of access ISPs, regional ISPs, tier-1 ISPs, PoPs, multi-homing, peering, and IXPs

Network Structure 5

Builds on top of Network Structure 4 by adding content-provider networks
